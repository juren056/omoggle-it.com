Is Omoggle Banned? Australia Block & Country Status

Updated July 20, 2026

Short answer: Omoggle is not banned worldwide, but as of June 10, 2026 it is blocked in Australia. The site's operators pointed to Australia's new under-16 social media restrictions as the reason for cutting off access there. It was the first national-level block of the platform.

Where Is Omoggle Blocked?

  • Australia — blocked since June 10, 2026. Access was pulled after Australia moved to restrict social media for under-16s. Australian users now see the site as unavailable.
  • Most other countries — still accessible. There is no global ban. However, regulators in several regions are watching the platform, so this can change.

Why Was Omoggle Blocked in Australia?

The block comes down to age and safety. Omoggle pairs random strangers on webcam and scores their faces — and its only age check is a single "I'm 18+" button with no real verification. That directly collides with Australia's tightening rules on minors and social platforms.

Australia's eSafety Commissioner said it was "concerned about the risks posed by randomised video chat platforms like Omoggle" and was monitoring the service's compliance with the Online Safety Act's codes and standards. An eSafety spokesperson noted that roulette-style apps randomly pairing strangers — especially those popular with children — "have been used by child sexual abuse offenders to groom Australian children."

Rather than build compliant age-verification, Omoggle's operators chose to block Australian access and cited the under-16 social media ban as the cause.

Is Omoggle Illegal?

No. Omoggle is a functioning website, not a scam, and it is not illegal in most countries. The Australian situation is a regional access block tied to online-safety regulation — not a criminal ruling. Adults elsewhere can still reach the site, with all the usual random-webcam risks that entails.

Will Omoggle Come Back in Australia?

Possibly, but only if the platform introduces genuine age verification or Australia's rules change. Experts caution that blocking one platform creates a false sense of security while near-identical sites keep appearing — so a return, or a wave of clones, is realistic. Parental awareness matters more than any single block.
